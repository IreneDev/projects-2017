Early Life

Grace Brewster Murray Hopper (1906 - 1992) was born in New York City. Hopper was her married surname. Her father was Walter Fletcher Murray, an insurance executive; and her mother was the mathematically talented Mary Campbell Van Horne Murray. There were three children in the family; Grace was the eldest. Grace was a big baby. She was 33 lbs. at 9 months! (And turned out to be a skinny adult.)


Her mother once left seven-year-old Grace unattended, and returned to discover her inquisitive daughter had made a tour of their home, collected seven clocks, and dismantled them all. Grace had started by dismantling just one clock to investigate its mechanism. Then she panicked because she didn’t know how to put it together again. So she got another clock, dismantled it… and you can guess the rest!


Education

College. In 1924, Grace matriculated at Vassar College, a private liberal arts college in Poughkeepsie, New York. Aged 17, Grace had already decided to major in mathematics. Her professors noticed she was unusually skilled at explaining difficult concepts in math and physics to other students. Grace graduated in 1928, aged 21, with a Bachelor of Arts degree in mathematics and physics.

Master’s and Marriage. Grace enjoyed academia and won a Vassar scholarship for further study. In 1928, her scholarship took her to graduate school at Yale University in New Haven, Connecticut. Two years later, Yale awarded her a master’s degree in Mathematics. Also that year she married Vincent Hopper, an academic whose field was literature.


Academia. The Great Depression had begun in 1928, and by 1931 over 8 million Americans were unemployed. And Grace Hopper was delighted to accept work as a mathematics assistant at Vassar College in 1931. Passionate about mathematics, Hopper submitted a thesis entitled The Irreducibility of Algebraic Equations to Yale and, age 27, got her Ph.D. in 1934. She continued working at Vassar, where a series of promotions carried her through the positions of instructor, assistant professor, and, in 1941, associate professor.


Career

USA Navy Rear Admiral. During World War II, Hopper was sworn into the United States Navy Reserve. In 1944 She was graduated at the Naval Reserve Midshipmen's School at Smith College in Northampton.


Grace Hopper was promoted to Commodore at the White House in 1983. President Ronald Reagan looks on. Her rank was reassigned in 1985 to Rear Admiral.

First Programmer of the Mark I. In 1944 Hopper graduated and was assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard University as a lieutenant, junior grade. She served on the Mark I computer programming staff headed by Howard H. Aiken. Hopper and Aiken coauthored three papers on the Mark I, also known as the Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator.

Creator of First Compiler A-0. In 1949, Hopper became an employee of the Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation for developing the UNIVAC I. She recommended that a new programming language be developed using entirely English words but it was not accepted for 3 years. By 1950s, when the company was taken over by the Remington Rand corporation her original compiler work was done and it was known as The A-0.

"Mother of COBOL". In 1959 Hopper served as a technical consultant to the committee that defined the new language COBOL. She developed validation software for COBOL and its compiler as part of a COBOL standardization program for the entire Navy. She also took over responsibility for standards testing of computer components and systems. Today these roles are carried out by the National Institute of Standards and Technology.
